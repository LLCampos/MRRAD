ABSTRACT BACKGROUND:
Although some evidence an association between a technology exposure in the literature and its dissemination in clinical practice, studies have evaluated such association. OBJECTIVE: To analyze whether the pattern of scientific publication on two competitive technologies used in the of disease (CAD) reflects what occurs in clinical practice. METHODS: The number of scientific articles published annually in the medical literature ( scientific interest in technology) on two technologies used in the of CAD was evaluated: electron beam (EBCT) and (MDCT). The number of countries that annually publish scientific articles about these technologies ( interest in the technology) was also analyzed. RESULTS: The EBCT showed a peak of " scientific interest" in 2001, with 127 published articles. this peak, the " scientific interest" by around 50% in 2008. In opposition, the " scientific interest" for MDCT progressively up to 2007, with 454 articles published in that year. The " scientific interest" by EBCT showed a peak in 2002, with 14 countries publishing about this technology. this peak, the " scientific interest" by almost 25% up to 2008, with countries publishing about this technology. In opposition, the " scientific interest" by MDCT progressively up to 2008, with 37 countries publishing articles about it. CONCLUSION: The medical scientific literature is compatible with the substitution of EBCT by MDCT in the of CAD. Therefore, methods are often developed with the of detecting CAD, from the electrocardiogram (ECG) to, more recently, the - (), () and the -detector (MDCT) itself15. However, while some technologies disseminate at a significant , being capable of modifying the paradigms concerning the attention to diseases, the developed technologies do always have a relevant clinical role and some of them simply do have significant clinical and thus rapidly pass from the of dissemination to the of obsolescence. It is evident, therefore, that the issue "technological dissemination" is currently very important and that the use of technology in healthcare constitutes a challenge for modern societies. Nonetheless, to the patterns of technology dissemination is an easy task due to the scarcity of data in this area and the little availability of these data in countries. The association between the production of knowledge and the use of technology has been described. Bruce J. Hillman10, as mentioned , that the curve of technological dissemination is preceded by a similar curve, representing the dissemination of knowledge on the technology itself. In a recent analysis, presented at the Congress of the Society of Medicine, in Toronto16, an association between the number of scientific articles published on a group of technologies used in Medicine and the use of these technologies in clinical practice was observed. In 2005 for instance, a correlation coefficient of 0.99 (p< 0.01) was observed between the number of scientific publications and the clinical use of technologies. In to deepen the evidence of this association between production of knowledge and use of technology, the study analyzed the pattern of exposure in the scientific literature of two technologies used in the of disease (EBCT and MDCT) and the existing data suggested that the EBCT became obsolescent because the manufacturers stopped its production6. The literature on these technologies were compatible with the substitution of EBCT by MDCT in practice, considering that the number of articles published on EBCT (" scientific interest" in the technology) as well as the number of countries that annually publish on the subject (" scientific interest" in the technology) started to decrease a years the of MDCT in the scientific literature. The reasons for this substitution must be diverse. While the performance of these technologies in the of the is quite comparable17, in the case of the coronary angiotomography, which provides coronary and , there is technological equality. Although the EBCT can also perform the angiotomography with , allowing the visualization of the coronary , the is degraded due to the in slice thickness18. Thus, the EBCT is practically exclusive for the of , a characteristic particularly problematic in Services that do not many cardiac assessments. Regardless of the causes that led to the substitution of EBCT by MDCT, the results herein presented corroborate the hypothesis made by other authors10, that the scientific production on a technology is associated to the use of this technology in clinical practice, thus reinforcing the idea that the analysis of scientific literature can act as the indicator for the use of a technology in clinical practice. The observation of this association is quite interesting, as measuring the use of a technology in clinical practice is a task, due to the little availability of data on such use. On the contrary, the of the exposure of the technology in the scientific literature can be carried out easily, through databases that are broadly available on the internet (PubMed, Lilacs and Embase, ISI, among others). Considering that the of the technological dissemination in clinical practice is quite important due to the costs involved in this practice, methods that allow estimating the behavior of the dissemination of different technologies can be useful to direct the investments to be made by healthcare providers and, therefore, prevent investments in technologies about to become obsolete. Additionally, they can also be useful to help the system payers (health insurance managers and providers) in the of which medical technologies have the potential to represent significant in costs of medical attention in the and term.   Conclusions The exposure of the medical technologies in the scientific literature seems to act as an estimate of the pattern of dissemination of these technologies in clinical practice and, in the study, it is compatible with the substitution of EBCT by MDCT in the of disease.
