ABSTRACT BACKGROUND:
Although some evidence an association between exposure technology in the scientific literature and its dissemination on clinical practice, studies have examined this association. The aim of this study is to analyze if the pattern of scientific publication about two competitive technologies, used in the evaluation of disease (CAD), reflects what occurs on clinical practice. METHODS: We evaluated the number of scientific articles published each year in the medical literature ( scientific interest on technology) on two technologies used in the evaluation of disease: and electron beam with detectors. It was also analyzed the number of countries that publish annual scientific articles about these technologies ( interest on technology). RESULTS: The electron beam (EBCT) showed a peak of " scientific interest" in 2001, with 127 published articles. this peak, the " scientific interest" by about 50% in 2008. In contrast, the " scientific interest" for the (MDCT) progressively until 2007, with 454 articles published in that year. The " scientific interest" by EBCT had a peak in 2002 with 14 countries publishing about this technology. this peak, the " scientific interest" declined by almost 25% until 2008, with countries publishing articles on this technology. In opposition, the " scientific interest" by MDCT progressively until 2008, with 37 countries publishing articles about it. CONCLUSION: The medical literature supports the substitution of EBCT by MDCT in the evaluation of the CAD. In this context, the use of technologies in the by area has greatly , with a rate of around 10% per year in the United States13. Specifically, with regard to the CAD, it is natural that the interest in the development of methods for the detection and monitoring of this is , since the prevalence of the disease is quite , and this is the main cause of mortality in some population groups14. Thus, methods are often developed with the purpose of detecting the CAD, from the electrocardiogram (ECG) to, most recently, the , the and tomography15. However, while some technologies spread in a significant speed, being able to the paradigms in the attention to diseases, the developed technologies do not always have an important clinical role and some simply do not show significant clinical applicability, and quickly pass the spreading to the obsolescence phase. It is evident, therefore, how important the "technological " theme is today, and the use of technology on health care is a challenge for modern societies. However, evaluating the technology spreading patterns is not easy, due to the paucity of data in this area and the availability of such data in countries. The association between the production of knowledge and the use of technology has been described. Bruce J. Hillman10, as mentioned, that the technological spreading curve is preceded by a similar curve, representing the dissemination of knowledge about the considered technology. In a recent analysis, presented at the congress of the Society of Medicine in Toronto16, we observed an association between the number of scientific articles published about a group of technologies used on nuclear medicine and the use of these technologies on clinical practice. In 2005, for example, the correlation index of 0.99 (p < 0.01) was verified among the number of scientific publications and the clinical use of the technologies. In to deepen the evidence of this relationship between knowledge production and use of technology, in this paper we analyze the pattern of exposure in the scientific literature of two technologies used in the evaluation of disease (EBCT and MDCT), and the data suggest that EBCT became obsolete, because the manufacturers have ceased their production6. The findings of the scientific literature on these technologies were compatible with the substitution of EBCT by MDCT on clinical practice, as the number of articles published on EBCT (" scientific interest" on technology) and the number of countries that annually publish on the subject (" scientific interest" on technology) began to decline a years the of MDCT in the scientific literature. Regardless of the causes that led to the of EBCT by MDCT, the results presented here support the hypothesis made by other authors10, that the scientific literature on technology is associated with the use thereof on clinical practice, therefore the idea that the analysis of the scientific literature can serve as an indicator of the use of clinical practice technology. The observation of this association is quite interesting, since measuring the use of technology on clinical practice is a difficult task, due to the availability of data on this use. Instead, the measurementof the exposure of the technology in scientific literature can be carried out simply, using databases widely available on the Internet (PubMed, Lilacs and Embase, ISI, etc.). Whereas the of technological on clinical practice is very important, due to the costs involved in this practice, methods that allow to estimate, in a siple way, the behavior of the spread of different technologies may be useful to direct the investments to be done by the providers of health service, and in this manner to avoid investments on technologies on the obsolescence routes. Besides, it may be also useful to help those who pay for the system (insurance managers and health insurers) in the evaluation of which medical technologies have the potential to represent significant on medical care costs in the and term.   Conclusions The exhibition of medical technologies in the scientific literature seems to serve as an estimate of the of these technologies on clinical practice and, in this work, is compatible with the substitution of EBCT by MDCT in the evaluation of disease.
